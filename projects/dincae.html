DINCAE: A Convolutional Network for Oceanographic Data Reconstruction

CNN B.Sc. Thesis Sep 2024 python julia tensorflow CNN

The problem

Sea Surface Temperature (SST) is fundamental for understanding the Earth's climate system. Satellite observations provide global SST data, but are frequently incomplete due to cloud cover, atmospheric interference, and sensor errors. The thesis analyzes DINCAE (Data INterpolating Convolutional Auto-Encoder), an algorithm designed to reconstruct these missing observations.

DINCAE 1.0 (Python / TensorFlow)

The first version is a convolutional autoencoder that handles missing data directly in the training loop. Missing values are represented with infinite variance (inverse = 0), effectively ignoring them during reconstruction. The network outputs both the reconstructed temperature field and an expected error variance for each pixel — making the reconstruction probabilistic rather than point-estimate only.

  • 4 convolutional encoding layers (16 → 54 filters, 3×3 kernel) with max/average pooling
  • 2 fully-connected bottleneck layers with 30% dropout
  • Skip connections between encoder and decoder to preserve fine-grained spatial information
  • Leaky ReLU activations throughout (avoids dead neurons)
  • Trained to minimize a negative log-likelihood loss assuming Gaussian output

DINCAE 2.0 (Julia)

The second version improves on v1.0 in several ways:

  • Double autoencoder with a refinement step, reducing vanishing gradient issues in deep networks
  • Sum-based skip connections instead of concatenation, halving output dimensionality and reducing overfitting risk
  • Multivariate inputs: SST, wind velocity, and chlorophyll data are fused into a single model
  • Non-grid data support: altimetric satellite data (collected along flight tracks) is handled via bilinear weight interpolation
  • Rewritten in Julia for faster execution and better numerical computing support

Practical experiments

DINCAE 2.0 was applied to SST data from the Strait of Gibraltar (2000–2020, 7,149 images at 149×106 px). Five different missing-data masks were applied to a trained model:

  • 10%, 25%, 50%, 90% uniform random masking
  • 25% blob masking (contiguous region removed)

Results showed that MSE increases with missing-data percentage, but remains manageable even at 90%. Strikingly, blob masking at 25% produced higher error than uniform masking at 90%, because contiguous missing regions lack any nearby observations for the network to interpolate from.
